Drink better coffee by cold brewing

2009-05-07

There are few things that beat sitting out on the patio of my favorite coffee shop (Rao’s Coffee) on a warm spring day, drinking an ice coffee. There are a few issues with this, I’m a student (poor) and going to Rao’s everyday adds up.
Turns out you can get that very same coffee shop taste with the help of some inexpensive supplies and a little bit of time, by cold brewing your coffee.

Step One - Supplies

First you’re going to need a container to steep the coffee in. I reused a glass bottle that originally contained Voss Sparkling water. The shape of the bottle doesn’t really matter, what matters is that it’s glass.

Second is the coffee beans. Pick up your favorite kind, just make sure it is a coarse ground. I’ve used both Rao’s Sumatra and Blue Bottle Coffee’s Bella Donovan, both having great full flavorful tastes.

Coffee filters are also needed.

Step Two - Process
According to Lifehacker:

1 cup of coarse-ground coffee and stir it into 4 1/2 cups of water

In my case that comes out to 12 tablespoons of coffee, working within a 1L bottle.

Add water, then put the cap on and shake up the mixture, until all the coffee has saturated with water.

Next stick the mixture into the fridge and let it sit for 12-24 hours, depending on how strong you like you’re coffee.

Use one of those coffee filters you bought and run the coffee once through. Smells good!

Step Three - Tasting

This is a highly caffeinated substance, due to the long steep time. To cut the strength of the coffee add either water, cream, or milk (you can drink it straight, but be prepared).

Do more with Google Gears

2009-05-06

Google Gears allows users to work on things found online, offline. Neat. Basically, this allows someone to read cache all their Gmail messages and read them when no longer connected to the cloud. Gears goes a little further, one can also compose email, to be queued and sent when a connection is found. This feature is not perfect though. Composing the text of the email goes off without a hitch, there is seemly no difference between composing an email on or offline. You cannot upload an attachment though. When attempting to, I was greeted with this (so Google) error message.

The error message does hint at it being a feature that Google possibly wants to add, noting how far Gmail has come. I think it’s an interesting feature, and suggest giving it a try. Just click on Settings then the Labs tab.
